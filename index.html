State of Good Repair

GRADE

C

State of Good Repair

Definition

This indicator averages the grades that Maryland receives from the American Society of Civil Engineers for roads, bridges, and transit.

Our Grade

The ASCE gave Maryland a B- for bridges, a C- for roads, and a C-for transit in its latest evaluation.

Reason

Transportation infrastructure was a huge factor in the economic growth in the U.S. and in greater Baltimore in the 19th and 20th Centuries and is critical to our competitiveness in the 21st Century. If we let it break down we choke the flow of goods and people that drive our regional economy.

Who Got an A?

Utah came closest with a B+ for roads, a B+ for bridges, and a B+ for transit in its most recent evaluation by the ASCE. The Utah Transportation Plan expresses the state’s attitude towards maintaining infrastructure with this quote: “Good roads cost less.”

Job Access by Transit

GRADE

D

Job Access by Transit

Definition

This indicator measures the percentage of our region’s jobs that a typical resident can get to in 60 minutes or less using forms of public transportation including buses and trains.

Our Grade

A typical Baltimorean can only get to 11% of the region’s jobs in under an hour using public transportation.

Reason

This indicator tells us whether the region’s public transportation system is helping connect the labor pool with employers. Many low-skill and mid-skill workers lack access to a car, effectively cutting them off from many job opportunities in the region. Programs like Vehicles For Change address that gap, but are not scalable enough to meet the massive need and must be supported in conjunction with better public transportation.

Who Got an A?

We believe that to earn an A a regional transportation system should provide access to at least 25% of its jobs in 60 minutes or less via public transportation. The Salt Lake City metro region where Utahans since the 1990’s have expanded the light rail system and overhauled their network of bus routes earns an A. A typical Salt Lake City resident get to 25.4% of the region’s jobs in under an hour on transit.

Mode Split

Definition

This indicator measures the percentage of commuters in the region who commute by walking, biking, transit, or carpooling.

Our Grade

Only 18% of our regional population commutes by walking, biking, transit or carpooling.

Reason

Driving is a convenient mode for many trips. However, the problem is when driving is the only feasible way to buy milk, visit a library or even get to a work out we generate too mufh air pollution and traffic congestion and get too little physical activity. This indicates that reasonable alternatives are not available. High-performing transportation systems provide opportunities for multi-modal transportation and do not force residents to use a particular mode.

Who Got an A?

We believe a 30% share of biking, walking, transit and carpooling is evidence of a robust multi-modal system. The Washington, DC metro region comes close with a 28% share, earning them a B+.

Isolated Neighborhoods

GRADE

F

Isolated Neighborhoods

Definition

This indicator measures the percentage of people in the region who suffer from spatial mismatch – the available transportation options do not efficiently connect them to job opportunities. A high percentage of the population living in isolated neighborhoods indicates that the transportation system is contributing to entrenched poverty and health disparities.

Our Grade

In Baltimore City 26% of the population live in neighborhoods where more than a quarter of workers commute at least 45 minutes one way to get to a job.

Reason

The Baltimore Neighborhood Indicators Alliance has found a strong correlation between neighborhoods where the highest percentage of workers commute at least 45 minutes and neighborhoods showing signs of distress including the highest levels of unemployment and poverty and the lowest life expectancies. The long commute time is an indicator of neighborhoods that are isolated from opportunities.

A Great Transportation Keeps You and Your Environment Healthy

Land Use

GRADE

B-

Land Use

Definition

This indicator divides the amount of land covered with buildings, parking lots and other impervious.

Our Grade

In the Baltimore-Towson metropolitan region there were 0.18 acres of developed land per capita in 2011.

Reason

The benefits of reducing the number of developed acres per capita include decreasing the loss of agricultural and natural resource land, mitigating pollution of the Chesapeake Bay and reducing the costs of providing infrastructure. Land is a finite resource. Transportation practices influence how efficiently we use it. A decline in the acres of developed land per capita indicates that the transportation system supports redevelopment of abandoned properties and compact forms of new development.

Who Got an A?

Among the metropolitan regions with similar population to Baltimore (between 2 million and 4 million people) San Diego earns a B+. In 2011 there were 0.16 acres of developed land per capita in the San Diego metropolitan region. Honorable mention goes to the Denver region where redevelopment and compact development activities between 2006 and 2011 brought developed land per capita into the B range.

Commute Time

GRADE

D

Commute Time

Definition

This indicator measures the average number of minutes that commuters in the region spend getting to work.

Our Grade

With an average commute time of 31 minutes, the Baltimore Region has the longest commute time among our peer regions and is the 6th worst of all metro areas in the nation. Transit riders suffer the most with an average commute time of 55 minutes, which is the second longest among our peer regions.

Reason

Longer commute times mean more time in sedentary positions (for those not biking or walking), more fuel consumption and more emissions. They mean less time for family or civic activities. Lack of physical activity is associated with obesity and numerous negative health outcomes. Longer commute times are bad for our bodies, our environment and our communities.

Who Got an A?

Minneapolis has an average commute time of 25 minutes that is below the national average (26 minutes) and has a transit commute time of 41 minutes that is also below the national average (49 minutes).

Congestion

GRADE

D

Congestion

Definition

This indicator measures the number of hours an average car commuter experiences travel delay due to congestion.

Our Grade

The average car commuter in the Baltimore region wastes 45 hours each year in traffic congestion.

Reason

Increased congestion means longer commute times, excess fuel consumption, and increased vehicle emissions. It is also indicative of inefficient land use policies, sprawl, and spatial mismatch between residents and employment centers.
